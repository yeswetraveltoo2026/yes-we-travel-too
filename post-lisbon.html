Lisbon on $120 a Day: A Real Budget Breakdown for Black Travelers

November 2024 · Lisbon, Portugal · 8 min read
🇵🇹

Lisbon has been on every "most affordable European city" list for the last five years, and while it's gotten more expensive (everywhere has), it still delivers more for your money than Paris, London, or Amsterdam. I spent 8 days there in November 2024 and averaged $118/day including accommodation — here's exactly what that looked like.

The Real Budget Breakdown

Accommodation: I stayed in Mouraria, Lisbon's oldest neighborhood and historically a neighborhood of immigration and cultural mixing — there's a reason it felt welcoming. I found a private room in a guest house for €65/night (~$70). Not an Airbnb — a family-run guest house where breakfast was included and the owner pointed me to spots that weren't on any app.

Food: Lisbon is extraordinarily generous with food costs if you eat where locals eat, which means: pastelarias (pastry shops) for breakfast at €2-3, lunch at a restaurant with a "prato do dia" (daily special) for €8-12 including a drink, and dinner at tascas (traditional taverns) for €15-20. Budget €30-40/day for food and you'll eat extremely well.

Transport: €1.50 per metro trip, €3 for a day pass on buses and trams (get the Viva Viagem card). Lisbon is also very walkable once you're in a neighborhood — I probably walked 6-8 miles a day just wandering.

Where to Actually Stay (Not the Bairro Alto)

Every travel blog sends you to Bairro Alto or Chiado. These are beautiful. They're also the most touristy and most expensive. Here's where I'd actually recommend staying as a first-time visitor who wants to experience the city rather than a curated version of it:

Mouraria: Historic, authentically multicultural, walking distance to everything. Best fado comes from here. The viewpoints (miradouros) are less crowded than in Alfama.

Intendente: Being called "up-and-coming" but it's been there. North African and Cape Verdean restaurants, local bars, less English on every menu. I ate some of my best meals here.

Almada (across the river): Technically not Lisbon, but a 10-minute ferry away and significantly cheaper for accommodation. Worth considering for longer stays.

The Part They Don't Put in Travel Guides

Lisbon has a complicated history with Africa and the African diaspora — Portugal was the first and last major European colonial power on the continent. There are monuments and statues celebrating the "Age of Discovery" that don't reckon with what was actually discovered, extracted, and destroyed. I wanted to sit with that history, not paper over it with pastéis de nata.

The Museu Nacional de Arte Antiga has a remarkable collection that includes art from across the Portuguese empire — some of it beautiful, some of it deeply implicating. The Berardo Collection Museum in Belém is world-class modern art. And then there's the experience of walking through a city where you'll hear Creole Portuguese, eat Cape Verdean cachupa, and understand viscerally that Black people have been part of this city's story for centuries.

The Tiles, Yes, Obviously the Tiles

Azulejos — the blue and white ceramic tiles that cover buildings, churches, and subway stations — are everything you've heard they are. The National Tile Museum (Museu Nacional do Azulejo) is worth every minute. But honestly the best tile-watching is free: just walk. Look up. Look at the walls. Some of the most extraordinary tilework is on the side of an ordinary apartment building in an ordinary neighborhood.

Quick Practical Notes

Best time to visit: May-June or September-October for weather and slightly thinner crowds. November was genuinely great — cool, not cold, and the city felt like it belonged to residents again.

Language: Portuguese, though English is widely spoken in tourist areas. Learning "obrigado/obrigada" (thank you) and "por favor" (please) is appreciated.

Getting there: TAP Air Portugal often has competitive transatlantic fares. JFK-LIS direct is about 7 hours.

I'd go back. The light alone is worth it.
